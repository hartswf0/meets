Dissertation Research

Thick Prompts as Cybernetic Molasses

W.F. Harts · 2024

Abstract

This practice-based dissertation investigates how thick prompts—densely layered, context-rich instructions—transform human-AI collaboration from mechanical query-response into genuine creative dialogue.

Drawing on Clifford Geertz's concept of thick description, Gregory Bateson's cybernetic theory of metacommunication, and Marshall McLuhan's distinction between hot and cool media, I argue that the quality of AI interaction depends not on prompt "engineering" but on prompt viscosity: the degree to which instructions carry embedded context, cultural weight, and relational thickness.

The archive presents 25 visual essays exploring the theoretical lineage and practical applications of viscous prompting.

Keywords: thick description, cybernetics, prompt engineering, human-AI collaboration, media theory, viscosity

Central Argument

The quality of human-AI collaboration depends not on prompt "engineering" but on prompt viscosity—the degree to which instructions carry embedded context, cultural weight, and relational thickness.

  1. Against thin prompts: Single-line instructions produce thin outputs because they lack the contextual friction that enables emergent meaning.
  2. Cybernetic molasses: Thick prompts function as high-viscosity feedback signals—they productively slow the system's response, creating space for genuine creative exchange.
  3. Cultural embedding: Effective prompts draw on thick description (Geertz), metacommunication (Bateson), and media temperature (McLuhan) to construct shared worlds rather than extract answers.
